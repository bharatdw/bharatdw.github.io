Introduction

The expectation from the fifth generation (5G) wireless communication system is that it will be able to handle about 1000 times more mobile data than todayâ€™s cellular systems. It will also become the backbone of the Internet of things (IoT) communication, linking up fixed and mobile devices, Sensor boards etc. The 5G cellular architecture follows a hierarchical concept, where channel reallocation efficiency is improved through the concepts of small cell networks. The small cell networks, which are known as macro-cellular network and pico-cellular networks based on the cell sizes, are efficient for low-range communication, such as IoT communication. To improve the energy efficiency and network manageability with low cost solutions, the 5G architecture also brings the concept of cloud based radio access networks (CRAN) where the baseband processor is physically segregated from radio frequency (RF) processor. To reduce the operational costs as well as to improve energy efficiency, the baseband processor is virtualized and is hosted over a cloud based framework so that the same cloud server can be reused for multiple baseband processing units (BBU), called the BBU pool over the cloud. This significantly reduces the cost for the RF transceivers that can be utilized to implement multiple macro base stations (called, macro eNodeB or macro-eNB) or pico base stations (called pico-eNB). Such macro-eNB and pico-eNB can be effectively utilized for IoT communication in multiple applications, like industrial control and automation, vehicular IoT or smart city context.

However from the initial implementation of the CRAN architecture, we realized that although such kind of architecture can provide an energy efficient, low operational cost, high availability and easy manageable solution for IoT communication over small cell networks, there are also several challenges that need to be addressed. First, many of the times IoT communications are delay and quality of service (QoS) sensitive, like in the case of industrial automation. However, the delay between the RF transceivers and BBU pool hosted on a remote cloud significantly affect the performance of the communication, in terms of throughput, delay, QoS and fairness. Therefore, the delay between the RF transceivers and cloud needs to be reduced for IoT communication over small cell. Second, the IoT communication infrastructure is itself heterogeneous by nature, where multiple frequency bands from various technologies (like 2.4 GHz or 5 GHz Wi-Fi communication or Bluetooth low energy communication) can be utilized at the client devices, along with the cellular communication. Therefore, the support at the base station needs to be heterogeneous and interoperable, so that the IoT clients can seamlessly communicate over the backbone.

To address the above two challenges, in this project, we plan to develop a new architectural framework for ubiquitous IoT communication, that leverage the advantages provided by CRAN and small cell based cellular communication, and brings the delay sensitivity, QoS and network heterogeneity solutions under the hood. We plan to develop a fog based hybrid radio access network framework, called Distributed Radio Network Architecture (DRoNA), which will apply the BBU pool virtualization concept at the edge, which is near to the small cell networks for IoT communication. Further, we plan to extend the near-the-edge BBU pool virtualization for multiple technology support via Cooperative Radio Resource Management (CRRM) and Collaborative Radio Signal Processing along with fronthaul compression techniques. We plan to implement a testbed for DRoNA, which can be utilized for prototyping ubiquitous IoT communications that has applications over diverse domains of cyber physical system, like industrial automation and control, vehicular safety and control management and smart city management.
